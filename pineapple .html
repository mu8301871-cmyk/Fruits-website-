Pineapple

A pineapple is a tropical fruit with a tough, scaly exterior and sweet, juicy yellow flesh. It is the most economically significant plant in the Bromeliaceae family, originating from South America and known for its crown of leaves. Pineapples are a good source of vitamin C, antioxidants, and manganese, and they contain an enzyme called bromelain, which has anti-inflammatory properties and can help tenderize meat. Description and characteristics Plant: An herbaceous perennial with a short stem and a rosette of spiky, sword-shaped leaves. Fruit: A single, cylindrical compound fruit that develops from many individual flowers fusing together. Flesh: Typically yellow, but varies by variety, and can be white in some types. Taste: Sweet and tart. Crown: The tuft of leaves at the top can be used for planting to grow a new pineapple plant. Nutritional information (per 1 cup, ~165g) Calories: 82 Carbohydrates: 22g Sugars: 16g Fiber: 2g Protein: 1g Vitamins: Good source of vitamin C (88 mg) and some vitamin A (1 mcg). Fat: 0g Culinary and other uses Food: Used in a variety of dishes, desserts, and juices. Meat tenderizer: The enzyme bromelain in pineapple helps break down tough meat proteins. Health benefits: The anti-inflammatory properties of bromelain may help relieve pain from osteoarthritis. Its antioxidants may also help fight free radicals, which are associated with cancer. Growing and history Origin: Indigenous to South America. Cultivation: Grown in tropical and subtropical regions worldwide, such as the Philippines, Indonesia, India, and China. History: In the 17th century, it was a symbol of luxury in Europe due to its rarity. Growth cycle: A single pineapple plant typically produces one fruit per year. Potential risks High in sugar: Juice is high in "free sugars," which are advised to be limited. Potassium: Contains potassium, so those taking certain heart medications (like ARBs) or with impaired kidney function should consume it in moderation and consult a doctor.
